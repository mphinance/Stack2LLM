Setting a stop loss on a winner is essentially the ultimate "Anti-Bagholder Insurance." It is the financial equivalent of leaving the party while you still have both your shoes and your dignity… if you ever brought it with you to begin with. I usually leave mine at home.

When you are up 10% in a single day, your brain starts whispering sweet lies about how you are a trading god and this stock is definitely going to the moon. The 5% stop loss is the sober friend who grabs the car keys. It ensures that even if the stock decides to pull a “Great Depression” move tomorrow morning, you still walk away with a win and a smile.

Friday January 9, the Phund made its second purchase, and what I’ll call first “real” one (as was more to have a placeholder, which we are rotating out of since we already hold NVDA) in , Roundhill’s Weekly pay ETF for which aims for extra returns on top of the share price increase each week.

Today, that stock/ETF is up 10% to $44, so we’re locking in a minimum 5% gains.

We accomplish this with the Stop Loss - in this case we’ll leave it Good Til Cancelled.

What this means is, at any point should our profits retrace from 10% ($44) all the way back to 5% ($42), a market order will be created to sell our BABW shares. This locks in that 5% gain, though it may be slightly lower depending on the spread at the time. If spread is an issue on a low-volume ticker, the Stop Loss Limit is the way to go, as it ensures you sell only at your desired price.
