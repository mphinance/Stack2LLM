The Recent Intel: ERP Hangovers and Market Share Battles

The headline numbers from the November earnings report were a bit of a psychological gut punch. While Clorox actually beat earnings per share expectations at 0.85, the 19% revenue drop and 17% organic sales decline sent the stock sliding toward its current 52-week low near 98.00.

The culprit is the ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system transition. Earlier in the year, retailers loaded up on inventory as a buffer, and they are now drawing that inventory down. Management expects this “inventory reversal” to be a 0.90 headwind to EPS for the full fiscal year 2026. Most concerning, however, was CEO Linda Rendle’s admission that the transition caused them to lose more market share in August and September than they anticipated. They are now in a dogfight to win back shelf space from private label brands that flourished while Clorox was dealing with shipping hiccups.

The Bear Case: Why the Stain Might Not Come Out

We have to look at why the “smart money” is currently sitting on its hands.

  • The Glad Buyout Liquidity Crunch: On January 31, 2026, the joint venture with P&G for Glad bags and wraps officially ends. Clorox is buying out P&G’s 20% stake at fair market value. This requires a significant cash outlay right when the company is already dealing with a negative equity position due to past share buybacks and a substantial debt load of over 2.7 billion.

  • Pricing Power Limits: In an inflationary environment where consumers are pinched, Clorox is finding it harder to pass on costs. If they push prices too high, they lose more share to generic brands. If they keep prices low, their margins (currently around 41.7%) will continue to contract.

  • The Dividend Ceiling: With a payout ratio near 80%, the dividend is safe for now, but the days of meaningful 5% to 7% annual raises are likely paused until the balance sheet is cleaned up.

The Investing Plan: Buying the Blood

Your long term goal here is to secure a high-quality income stream at a generational yield.

  • Margin of Safety: At 98.00, the stock is trading at roughly 16 times forward earnings, which is a steep discount to its 5-year average of 23 times. With a 5.05% dividend yield, you are getting a massive yield spread over the S&P 500.

  • Accumulation Strategy: Buy 40% of the total desired position here at 98.00. Save the next 30% for a “flush” toward 92.00, which would represent a terminal valuation level. Keep the final 30% for when the stock moves back above 108.00, confirming that the ERP transition is fully behind them.

  • The Long Thesis: You are betting that the “household staple” status of brands like Hidden Valley and Burt’s Bees is temporarily obscured by a technical accounting shift (the ERP drawdown) rather than a permanent loss of brand power.

This is for the capital appreciation side of your brain, looking for a mean reversion.

  • Setup: Clorox is deeply oversold with an RSI hovering near 36. It has formed a potential double bottom at the 96.66 level.

  • Entry: Buy a starter trade position at 98.20 or on any dip toward 97.00.

  • Stop Loss: 94.50. If the stock loses the 95.00 psychological support, the downward momentum is too strong to fight, and we exit to protect capital.

Summary and Risk Reward Analysis

The risk to reward ratio on the trading side is approximately 3.5 to 1. You are risking about 3.70 per share to gain roughly 11.00 per share on a move back to the moving average.

From a fundamental “Margin of Safety” perspective, the Free Cash Flow (FCF) yield is currently sitting at a healthy 5.4%. In the 2025 fiscal year, Clorox generated 761 million in FCF, which is more than enough to cover the 618 million in dividends paid. This suggests that while the earnings reports look messy, the actual “cash in the door” remains robust. The investment risk is not that the company goes bankrupt, but that it becomes a “dead money” asset for another 12 months as it fights for market share. However, for investors like us, getting paid 5% annually to wait for a 25% valuation recovery is a trade we take most days of the week.
