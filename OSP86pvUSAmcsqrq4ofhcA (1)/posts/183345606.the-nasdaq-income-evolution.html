Total Real Returns Comparison

For a long time, if you wanted income from the Nasdaq 100, you bought and called it a day. It was the “original” high yield tech play, launching way back in 2013. But the market has moved on, and if you are still holding the old guard, the data suggests you might be leaving significant growth on the table.

Looking at recent total return charts, the performance gap between these funds is staggering. We are no longer just choosing between “growth” and “income.” We are choosing between different philosophies of how to harvest volatility.

The Three Generations of Nasdaq Income

1. The First Gen: The Passive ATM Model (QYLD)

QYLD is the elder statesman. Its strategy is simple: it buys the Nasdaq 100 and sells “at-the-money” (ATM) calls on 100% of the portfolio.

The problem? It caps every ounce of upside. In a bull market, you are essentially watching the party through a window. Over years, this has led to “NAV erosion,” where the share price slowly bleeds out because the fund captures all the downside but none of the recovery.

2. The Second Gen: The Active Disruption (JEPQ, QYLG)

3. The Third Gen: The Sophisticated Alpha (QQQI, GPIQ)

This is the “new school” that is currently winning. Funds like use call spreads rather than just selling single calls.

By selling a call and buying a further out-of-the-money call, QQQI keeps a “window” of growth open. If the Nasdaq goes on a tear, QQQI captures a portion of that run up that QYLD and even JEPQ might miss. Add in the tax efficiency of Section 1256 contracts, and you have a strategy that looks almost as good as straight growth in certain windows, but with a massive monthly distribution.

Market Conditions: Who Wins When?

The Ripping Bull Market

Winner: (Growth) / Runner Up: QQQI
If tech is flying, QQQI is the best income play. Because of the call spread strategy, it doesn’t hit a “hard ceiling” the way the others do. It follows the growth curve much more closely than the older models.

The Sideways or “Chop” Market

Winner: JEPQ / GPIQ
When the market is moving in a range, active management shines. These funds can adjust their “coverage” based on how much premium is available. They harvest the “rent” from the volatility without needing the stocks to actually move up.

The Volatile Downturn

Winner: QQQI
The premiums collected act as a buffer. While it will still go down in a crash, the “lower Beta” (relative risk) means it usually drops less than the pure Nasdaq index. It is the “smoother ride” for people who can’t stomach a 30% drawdown.

The 0DTE Gamble (QQQY, I-QQQ)

Then there are the “wacky” newcomers using 0DTE (Zero Days to Expiration) options. These funds, like QQQY, are essentially trying to capture the “daily rent” of the market.

While the yields are eye popping (often 20% to 50% or more), they are extremely dangerous in high volatility. Because they reset every single day, they are prone to “volatility decay.” If the market gaps down overnight, they can lose value that is very difficult to recover. These are best used as “income boosters” in a very specific, stable market, not as core holdings.

The Verdict: Don’t Get Trapped by Yield

By using more sophisticated option Greeks, these new funds have figured out how to give you the check without killing the golden goose.

If you are still in QYLD, you are using 2013 technology in a 2025 market. It might be time to look at the “Generation 3” funds that respect the growth potential of the underlying tech while still paying you to stay in the game.
