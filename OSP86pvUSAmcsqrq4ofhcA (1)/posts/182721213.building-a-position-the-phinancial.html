Building a significant position in a stock is rarely a one click event. Most traders are taught to "scale in" using equal increments, such as buying $2,000 worth of shares five times to reach a $10,000 goal, for example. How often does that actually work out? Investors, sure, but rarely are traders able to do this, and rarely does it make sense.

What follows are a series of mathematical sequences to ensure every buy order is unique, based on fibs. This makes the process more strategic and allows you to adjust your exposure based on how well you know the stocks’ personality.

As always, not financial advice and all that. You don’t even have to use this exactly - make up your own! I write this to challenge you to explore additional ways to trade successfully that work FOR YOU, while maintaining discipline.

I use these two actual techniques myself. One is the new girl I’m getting to know, so I’m taking it a little slow - the other is someone I’ve hooked up with before, and she’s already in a position I like and am familiar with.

The Fibonacci “Test Drive”: The New Girl

When you are looking at a brand new stock, there is a period of “getting to know each other.” You don’t necessarily want to dive in head first before seeing how the price action treats you. For these scenarios, a Fibonacci build (using the ratios 1, 2, 3, 5, and 8) is ideal.

By skipping the duplicate “1” in the traditional sequence, you ensure every buy is larger than the last. This creates a “19x” total position. If your target is $10,000, your base unit is roughly $525.

Feel free to adjust this for the situation, and I often do. Sometimes I don’t skip the duplicate 1 so my buys are 1,1,2,3,5 creating a “12x” total position, for example.

You start small with a $525 “starter” position. If the stock proves your thesis correct by moving higher, you add $1,050. As your confidence grows and the trend confirms, your buys increase to $1,575, then $2,625, and finally a “full position” top off of $4,225.

This method is mathematically elegant because it forces you to "earn" your largest position. You only have your biggest money at risk once the stock has already shown it can move in your direction and you’re familiar with how it moves.

The “Old Flame”: Inverted Pyramid

There are times when a “test drive” isn’t necessary. Perhaps this is a stock you have traded successfully for years, or maybe it’s “the one” you have been stalking for months, waiting for it to finally hit a specific price floor or her to become single.

When you have high conviction or a deep history with a company, the Inverted Pyramid is the superior tool. You simply reverse the Fibonacci logic to 8, 5, 3, 2, 1.

Instead of starting small, you lead with your heaviest buy of roughly $4,210 right at the start. This puts the bulk of your capital to work at what you believe is the best possible price. As the stock begins its recovery or breakout, you add $2,630, then $1,580, and continue tapering down.

By the time the stock has moved significantly higher, you are only adding small "top off" amounts ($1,050 and $530). This keeps your average cost much lower than the other methods, ensuring that if the stock eventually hits a plateau, your profit margin is already well protected.

Strategy Over Repetition

Whether you are cautiously approaching a new name or aggressively pouncing on a long awaited entry, these sequences keep your trading dynamic.

  • The Fibonacci Build protects your capital while you learn the stock.

  • The Inverted Pyramid maximizes your gains on high conviction plays.

By using these methods, you turn the act of buying into a strategic advantage, ensuring your money enters the market with a specific purpose rather than just filling a quota.
