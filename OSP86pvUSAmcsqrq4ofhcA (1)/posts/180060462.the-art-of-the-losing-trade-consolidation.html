I call this strategy “How to turn winners into losers by holding too long, then turning them back into break-evens because I refuse to accept defeat.”

The Story - I was staring at the option chain this morning, annoyed at the sea of red on my 3x Jan ‘27 $70 calls. They weren’t exactly printing.

Then I noticed something while scanning the strikes. The bid on my 3 losing contracts was sitting at $6.80. A few strikes down, the deep ITM $60 calls were sitting at $9.30.

My brain did the quick math: 3 of these equals 2 of those.

The Fix - Rather than staring at the red and hoping $BTC magically rockets to 100k by Friday to save these specifically, I’m managing the risk.

1. Rolled one: Already moved one of the 70s to a better spot (the $60 strike you see in the green).

2. Rolling the second: Tomorrow, I’m taking another one of those dying 70s and rolling it deep.

3. The Sacrifice: The 3rd contract? I’m selling it for a loss.

The Math: Quality Over Quantity

You might ask why I’d sell a contract just to hold fewer of them. It comes down to the cash flow.

1. The Liquidation (The Losers): I have 3 contracts at the $70 strike. 3 contracts x $6.80 = $2,040 Total Equity

2. The Acquisition (The Fix): I’m consolidating into the $60 strike (Deep ITM/Safer). 2 contracts x $9.30 = $1,860 Total Cost

3. The Net Result: $2,040 (Credit) - $1,860 (Debit) = +$180 Net Cash Credit

By selling the 3 risky contracts and buying 2 safer ones, I’m actually putting $180 back in my pocket (cash-wise) while significantly improving my probability of profit. I bought myself a reset button without putting new capital at risk.

Managing Leverage: The Greek Shift

Why trade 3 contracts for 2? Isn’t that less leverage? Yes, and that’s the point. I am trading “lotto ticket” leverage for “sustainable” leverage.

The 70 Strike ($6.80): Lower Delta. These need $IBIT to move hard and fast to stay alive. They are bleeding Theta (time value) faster because they are closer to At-The-Money.

The 60 Strike ($9.30): Higher Delta. These act more like owning the shares. They have more intrinsic value. If $IBIT creeps up slowly, these still print. If $IBIT stays flat, these bleed less.

I am reducing my total exposure from 300 shares (controlled via option) to 200 shares, but I have a wider safety net.

Sometimes trading isn’t about making a million dollars on every trade. Sometimes it’s about looking at a mistake, doing some quick math, and restructuring the trade so you live to fight another day.
