The traditional 4% safe withdrawal rule for retirement is fine, but relies on you eventually having to sell your babies to meet cash requirements. We here at Momentum Phinance (like how I can say “we” now and sound like a big deal? I feel like a promotion too… should I be CEO or President today?) prefer the passive income approach, which means focusing on a far sexier number: your Yield on Cost (YOC).

YOC is the annual dividend you receive relative to your original purchase price, making it the only metric that truly reflects the income return on your invested capital.

My personal target is to get every core dividend position to a minimum 5% YOC to have a little leeway - but this way we don’t have to actually sell stock.

So how do you get this income stream to stand up on its own two feet? You put the Dividend Reinvestment Plan, or DRIP, to work. Think of DRIP as your investment’s ultimate wingman. Every dividend that hits the account isn’t taken out; it’s immediately used to buy more shares, which then generate even more dividends. It’s compounding having its way with your portfolio. This is the accumulation phase, where we let the power of time and dividend growth accelerate our share count until that 5% YOC target is met. The DRIP switch stays firmly ON—no interruptions—until we’ve reached that required dividend amount based on our basis.

Once a stock, like from my screenshot, hit that 5% YOC milestone, we flip the script: DRIP OFF. This is the cash flow transition. That dividend income is now generating enough yield on your original investment that it’s ready to go solo. Initially, this new cash flow gets put to work elsewhere—diversifying into a new sector, funding options trades, or beefing up a position that still hasn’t hit its 5% threshold. But the end game is retirement. When you generate a 5% cash return purely from dividends, you’ve not only exceeded the 4% rule, you’ve done it without ever having to liquidate a single share of stock. That’s the difference between relying on capital gains and truly retiring on pure, passive income.
